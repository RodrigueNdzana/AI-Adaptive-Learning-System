# Reflection

Working on Agile planning for this project revealed both practical and personal challenges. One of the most significant difficulties was prioritising features while balancing ambition with realism. As the sole stakeholder in this context, I had to confront my natural tendency to want all features implemented at once. The system includes advanced functionalities such as AI recommendations, accessibility tools, dashboards, and reporting. However, Agile emphasises delivering value early through a Minimum Viable Product (MVP). This required me to carefully select only the most essential features, ensuring that the system remains functional while avoiding unnecessary complexity in the initial stages.

Another challenge involved breaking down large system requirements into smaller, manageable user stories. Certain features, such as AI-based recommendations and accessibility support, are inherently complex and multifaceted. Translating these into simple, clear, and testable user stories required deliberate effort to ensure alignment with the INVEST principles. In particular, ensuring that each story was independent and testable often required separating components that initially appeared interconnected. This process improved my ability to think critically about system design and modularity.

Effort estimation also proved to be challenging. Without the support of a development team or access to historical project data, assigning story points required making informed assumptions about complexity and effort. This experience highlighted the subjective nature of estimation in Agile environments and reinforced the importance of experience in improving accuracy. I relied on relative estimation techniques, comparing tasks against one another rather than attempting to predict exact completion times.

Sprint planning introduced an additional challenge related to discipline and scope management. There was a strong temptation to include more user stories within a single sprint in an effort to accelerate progress. However, Agile principles emphasise sustainable development and realistic workloads. I had to consciously limit the sprint scope to prevent overcommitment, which could negatively impact both quality and completion rates. This experience reinforced the importance of maintaining focus and adhering to planned sprint goals.

Another key consideration was balancing technical and user-focused requirements. Features such as encryption and system security are essential for system integrity, yet they do not always provide immediate, visible value to end users. Deciding how to prioritise these features required a broader understanding of both technical reliability and user experience. This highlighted the need to balance backend robustness with frontend usability in system design.

Overall, this exercise significantly deepened my understanding of Agile methodology. It demonstrated that Agile is not merely about speed, but about delivering value incrementally, adapting to constraints, and maintaining a clear focus on user needs. Additionally, it emphasised the importance of self-management when working independently. The processes of prioritisation, estimation, and sprint planning required me to think from both a developer’s and a product owner’s perspective, which is critical for developing effective, user-centered systems.
